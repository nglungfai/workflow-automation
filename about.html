How we practice AI

What this site is, how it's made, and why it's a live demonstration rather than a write-up

This site is a working portfolio of agentic workflow engineering. It began as the internal knowledge base for using Claude Cowork on my product team, and it remains the clearest demonstration of how we operate: everything described on these pages runs in production, including the pipeline that publishes this very page. We don't consume AI second-hand. We run a full pipeline on it. Agentic search sweeps the AI literature across every topic that touches our work. A living knowledge base distills what matters into the AI Reading List and hands-on tutorials. Agentic workflows turn those ideas into shipped work — every week. Research in, working systems out: that's the standard we hold ourselves to as practitioners.

Research in, workflows out

Discovery is automated and broad. The pipeline starts with coverage no manual reading habit can match: search agents sweep new research papers, engineering write-ups, and practitioner guides across the full topic surface of our stack — prompting and context engineering, agent architectures, retrieval and embeddings, evaluation, applied machine learning — and screens every candidate against one question: does this change how we build or operate?

What survives becomes a knowledge base, not a link dump. Every entry in the reading list carries a note on how it applies to our work and cross-links to a tutorial showing the idea running in our own workflows. A monthly curation cycle retires stale entries and promotes what's proven. Theory is indexed against practice, so nothing sits on a shelf.

Then the ideas go to work. The same research feeds the workflows we run every day — packaged skills for bulk segment creation, scheduled reviews that maintain this very site, connector pipelines that turn meetings into action items and drafts into shipped documents. The distance from a paper we read to a workflow we run is measured in days, not quarters.

What the team gets back

The pipeline is judged by what it hands back to people. Agents absorb the mechanical work — clicking, assembling, formatting — while humans keep the judgment: what ships, what changes, what's next. The hours in the table below didn't vanish; they moved from production to decisions. Real tasks from our own team — measured, not marketed. Full context and more examples in Getting Started:

How this site is made

This site isn't written about agentic workflows — it is one. Every page you're reading was drafted, cross-linked, and committed to production by Cowork. A person describes the deliverable; the agent writes the HTML, pushes the commit, and the change is live in about a minute. Humans hold exactly two gates: review before anything publishes, approval before any maintenance change ships.

Maintenance runs on agents too. A scheduled agent audits the site monthly — stale entries, broken links, drifted claims — and files a report. It proposes; it never decides.

And the system compounds. Every reviewer correction and every failure the pipeline hits is encoded back into the skills and memory the agents run on — a deployment quirk becomes a documented recovery step, a style correction becomes a rule the next draft follows automatically. The same mistake doesn't ship twice, and each publish cycle runs on everything learned from the last. That's the operating model we advocate everywhere: agentic, self-improving, running in production.
